Dropout & Regularization

Why overfitting happens, and how dropout + L2 penalties keep models honest
Dropout rate: 0.50 · Neurons active: -
The Big Idea
Don't memorize — generalize
A model with too many parameters can memorize the training data instead of learning patterns. It performs perfectly on data it has seen, but fails on new data. Regularization forces the model to stay simple: dropout randomly silences neurons, L2 penalizes large weights.

Dropout Rate

p (drop probability)0.50
Every training step, each neuron is randomly turned off with probability p. This prevents neurons from co-adapting. It's like a team where every day random members are absent — everyone learns to work independently.

Model Complexity

Polynomial degree3
L2 regularization (λ)0.00
Low degree = underfit (too simple). High degree = overfit (memorizes noise). L2 penalty shrinks weights, making the model smoother.

Why It Works

Regularization introduces a bias toward simplicity. Without it, models will use their full capacity to fit noise. Real-world test: models with regularization have higher training loss but lower test loss — which is what we actually care about.

In Real Training

Modern LLMs use weight decay (equivalent to L2) and dropout (typically 0.1 in transformer layers). Other techniques: early stopping, data augmentation, label smoothing, mixup. All fight the same enemy: overfitting.
