The big idea
Softmax makes valid probabilities out of raw scores.
A network emits unnormalised logits zi ∈ ℝ. Softmax exponentiates (making every value positive) and divides by the total (making them sum to 1). Temperature τ rescales the logits before the exp — small τ sharpens, big τ flattens.

Top-k keeps the k highest-probability classes; top-p keeps the smallest prefix whose cumulative probability is at least p. The kept set is the intersection of the two, then renormalised. This is how most LLMs sample.

True class (for cross-entropy)

Cross-entropy against a one-hot target y is just −log₂ py. When the model is confidently correct it approaches 0; when it's confidently wrong it blows up to ∞.

Why softmax?

exp is the unique (positive, differentiable, monotone) function whose ratios depend only on differences: exp(zi)/exp(zj) = exp(zi−zj). That's why softmax is translation-invariant — adding 1000 to every logit changes nothing. It's also why subtracting max(z) before the exp is the canonical numerical-stability trick (see the code).
Entropy H(p) = −Σ pᵢ log₂ pᵢ measures uncertainty in bits. Perplexity 2H is the "effective number of equally-likely choices." KL divergence from uniform is log₂ n − H(p) — zero for uniform, maxes out when p is a point mass. All three move in lockstep as you drag logits.
Top-k caps tail length; top-p caps tail mass. On a flat distribution (many plausible classes) top-p keeps more than k. On a peaked distribution top-k keeps more than p. Most LLMs use both: take the intersection, renormalise, sample. This page shows the cut lines on the sorted CDF so you can see the kept nucleus.
With logits (z₁, 0), softmax gives (e^z₁, 1)/(e^z₁ + 1), which is exactly the sigmoid of z₁ for the first class. Binary classifiers use this shortcut because the second class is redundant: its probability is always 1 − p₁.
