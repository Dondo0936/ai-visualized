The Big Idea
LLMs don't see letters — they see tokens
Before an LLM processes your text, it's chopped into tokens (word pieces). Each token becomes a number. Common words = 1 token. Rare words get split into pieces. This is why "unhappiness" might become ["un", "happiness"] or even ["un", "happy", "ness"].

Why Split Words?

If every word got its own token, the vocabulary would be millions of entries. Instead, modern LLMs use ~50,000 tokens by splitting rare words into common pieces. This lets the model handle words it has never seen before — it recognizes the pieces.

The Algorithm (BPE)

Byte Pair Encoding starts with individual characters, then repeatedly merges the most common pair. Over millions of documents, "th" becomes a token, then "the", then " the". Eventually it knows full words AND their pieces.

What you see here is a simplified simulation — real GPT tokenizers use ~100K trained tokens.

Fun Facts

• GPT-4 has 100,277 unique tokens
• Leading spaces matter: " hello" and "hello" are different tokens
• Emoji often take multiple tokens
• Your API costs are measured in tokens, not words
• Non-English languages usually use more tokens per word

Connection to the Model

Each token ID looks up a learned embedding vector. Those vectors flow through transformer layers, get attended to each other, and finally produce logits that become the next token's probability via softmax.
