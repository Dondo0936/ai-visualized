Key Concepts

In machine learning, the loss function measures how wrong a model's predictions are. Each point on this surface represents a specific set of model parameters (weights). Height = error. Training a neural network = finding the lowest point.

Real networks have millions of dimensions. We show 2 so you can see it. But all the ideas — curvature, saddle points, local minima, optimization momentum — generalize.
The gradient ∇f is the vector of partial derivatives: (∂f/∂x, ∂f/∂y). It points in the direction of steepest ascent. Its magnitude is how steep. The negative gradient is the direction of fastest decrease — which is why all gradient methods step "downhill."
The Hessian is a matrix of second derivatives: how does the gradient change as you move? Its eigenvectors (purple arrows when enabled) show the principal axes of curvature. The eigenvalues say how steep in each direction.

If both eigenvalues are positive → local minimum. Both negative → local maximum. Mixed signs → saddle point. This is why Adam works: it approximates the inverse of the second moments per-parameter.
SGD follows the raw gradient. Simple but gets stuck in ravines (oscillates across walls).
Momentum accumulates past gradients. Like a heavy ball — dampens oscillation, builds speed in consistent directions.
NAG (Nesterov) looks ahead: computes gradient at the *predicted* next position, then corrects.
Adagrad divides the step by √(sum of squared gradients). Adapts per-parameter but the denominator only grows.
RMSProp uses an EMA of squared gradients instead — fixes Adagrad's monotone decay.
Adam combines momentum + RMSProp + bias correction. Go-to for most deep learning.
AdamW = Adam with decoupled weight decay. Used in GPT, LLaMA, virtually every modern LLM.
Constant: α fixed. Simple, surprisingly often good enough.
Step-decay: Halve α every N steps. Classical, still widely used for CV.
Cosine: Smoothly anneal α following a half-cosine. Trained LLaMA, used in most modern transformer training.
Warmup + Cosine: Start small, ramp up linearly, then cosine decay. Stabilizes early training; standard for transformers.

See the third chart at the bottom-right to watch α(t) live.
Every neural network is trained with a variant of gradient descent. GPT-4 was trained with AdamW + cosine LR decay + warmup, on ~25K GPUs, for months. The exact same operation you see here runs trillions of times per training run.

What's different at scale: mini-batches (compute gradient on a subset, hence "stochastic"), distributed training (gradients averaged across GPUs), mixed-precision (fp16/bf16), gradient clipping, and exotic schedules. But the core loop is identical.
