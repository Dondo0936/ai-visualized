The Big Idea
Every neuron starts with a line
The equation y = mx + b is the most important equation in AI. Every single neuron in a neural network computes a weighted sum of its inputs (that's mx) plus a bias (that's b), then decides what to output. Before we can understand deep learning, we need to feel what m and b do.

Slope (m) — "how steep"

m 1.00
m = 1.00 — For every 1 step right, the line goes up 1 step. A positive slope tilts up-right. In a neuron, m is the weight — how much this input matters.

Y-Intercept (b) — "where it crosses"

b 0.00
b = 0.00 — The line crosses the y-axis at 0. In a neuron, b is the bias — it shifts the decision threshold up or down.

The Bridge to Neural Networks

A single neuron computes: output = activation(w*x + b). That's just y = mx + b wrapped in an activation function! With multiple inputs: output = activation(w1*x1 + w2*x2 + ... + b). That's a dot product plus bias. Stack thousands of these, and you have a neural network.
Training = finding the best m and b (or millions of weights). You start with random values, measure the error, then nudge m and b in the direction that reduces error. That nudging process IS gradient descent. The derivative tells you which direction to nudge.
