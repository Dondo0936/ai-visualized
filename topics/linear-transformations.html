The big idea
A matrix is a function that moves the grid.
The two columns of a 2×2 matrix tell you where the basis vectors î = (1,0) and ĵ = (0,1) land. Every other point gets carried along: (x,y) goes to x·î_new + y·ĵ_new. Scaling, rotation, shear, reflection — all of them are just pairs of column choices.

Why these numbers matter

The unit square (1×1) maps to a parallelogram. Its signed area is det(M) = ad − bc. det = 1 preserves area, |det| > 1 expands, |det| < 1 shrinks, det = 0 collapses onto a line (no area left), and det < 0 means the orientation flipped — î and ĵ swapped sides. A matrix is invertible iff det ≠ 0.
An eigenvector v of M satisfies M·v = λ·v: M only stretches it by a factor λ (the eigenvalue). On the canvas these show in green. When tr² − 4·det < 0 the eigenvalues are a complex pair — the matrix is a pure rotation or rotation+scale, and no real eigenvector exists (nothing stays put).
For a vector v, (M₂·M₁)·v = M₂·(M₁·v). The right-most matrix acts on v first. So the label "M₂·M₁" means "apply M₁, then M₂" — the order on the page is the opposite of the order applied. Flip the "order" chip above to see the difference: M₂·M₁ ≠ M₁·M₂ in general.
Every dense layer is h = φ(W·x + b). The matrix W is a linear transformation — in high dimensions it does exactly what the 2×2 does here: rotates, scales, shears, and projects. Activation φ adds the non-linearity. Stack many of these and you can approximate any reasonable function. The intuition you build playing with this 2×2 generalises.
